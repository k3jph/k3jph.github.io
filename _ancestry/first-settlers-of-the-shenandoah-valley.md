The [First Settlers of the Shenandoah
Valley](http://www.firstsettlersshenandoahvalley.com/) (FSSV) is a lineage society
that recognizes descent from individuals who settled in the Shenandoah Valley
prior to 31 December 1779. The society preserves memory of the early colonial
settlers who transformed the Valley from a contested wilderness into a
productive agricultural frontier, and it documents the documentary evidence that
connects their descendants to that founding generation. Membership requires
proof of direct lineal descent and evidence of the qualifying ancestor's
presence in the Valley within the qualifying period.

## William Saffer

My qualifying ancestor is [William
Saffer](https://www.wikitree.com/wiki/Saffron-14) (also recorded as Safer and
Saffron; born circa 1734, died before 12 January 1792 in Shenandoah County,
Virginia), an early settler of the Shenandoah Valley whose land holdings are
documented across multiple Lord Fairfax grants and Virginia county records
spanning more than three decades. He is my eighth great-grandfather.

The documentary record for William is thin, as was typical for modest
landholders of his era and region. No birth record survives, his parentage is
unknown, and his wife's name has not been recovered. What does survive is a
paper trail of land and law that establishes him as a continuous and documented
presence in the Valley from 1757 until his death.

### The Northern Neck Proprietary and Lord Fairfax

To understand William's land tenure, it is necessary to understand the unusual
legal framework governing land in the northern Shenandoah Valley in the
eighteenth century. The Northern Neck Proprietary, also called the
Fairfax Grant, was a land grant first contrived by the exiled English King
Charles II in 1649 and encompassing all the lands bounded by the Potomac and
Rappahannock Rivers in colonial Virginia, constituting up to five million acres.
By 1719, these lands had been inherited by Thomas Fairfax, 6th Lord Fairfax of
Cameron. The Privy Council in 1745 secured the northern Shenandoah
Valley to Fairfax, and he returned permanently to Virginia in 1747, building an
estate known as Greenway Court in what is now Clarke County.

The practical consequence for settlers like William was that acquiring legal
title to land in the Valley required dealing with Lord Fairfax's land office
rather than with the colonial government in Williamsburg. Fairfax issued grants
in exchange for quit-rents, the modest annual payments that constituted the
proprietary's revenue. A Fairfax grant was not merely a purchase; it was entry
into a feudal-adjacent system of tenure that defined land ownership in the
Valley until the Revolution rendered the proprietary defunct.

### Land Grants and Settlement

William Saffer received a land grant of 249 acres in 1757 in
Frederick County, Virginia. His land was located in the Scotch-Irish settlement
area of Frederick County, with surveys for his tract located adjacent south of
Overall, referred to as Over Run or Caleb's Run. This initial grant
establishes his presence in the Valley by 1757 at the latest and provides the
basis for his qualification as a First Settler. The application record for this
membership cites Doc. 49, a land grant to neighboring settler Shadrack Parlour
from Lord Fairfax dated 1760, and Doc. 50, the 1771 Fairfax grant to William
himself, together confirming his continuous foothold in the region across those
years.

The 1771 grant represents a second, or supplemental, acquisition and confirms
that William remained an active landholder in the Valley well into the period
leading up to the Revolution. By this point the county structure had been
reorganized, and what had been Frederick County was gradually subdivided; the
area near Caleb's Run eventually fell within Shenandoah County, where William's
estate would be probated two decades later.

An 1778 deed records that Adam Cunningham obtained land adjacent to
William Saffer's holdings and that of Ephraim Leith, placing William among an
identifiable cluster of neighboring landholders in the South Fork region.
This is precisely the kind of incidental mention that anchors a lightly
documented ancestor in the local geography and confirms that his land was
occupied and known to his neighbors well into the Revolutionary period.

### The Census Records of 1783 and 1785

William appears in two enumeration records that bracket the end of the
Revolutionary War. He was included in the 1783 Hite enumeration as head
of household, and in the 1785 enumeration census as head of family with seven
people in one dwelling, as recorded in *A History of Shenandoah County* by John
W. Wayland. Seven persons in a single household in 1785 is suggestive of a
multi-generational or extended family structure, though the individual members
cannot be identified with certainty from the enumeration alone. It confirms,
however, that William survived the Revolution and remained resident in
Shenandoah County with a substantial household at that date.

### The Estate of William Saffer

William died sometime before 12 January 1792, when his estate was inventoried
in Shenandoah County. His probate appears in the Virginia Wills and
Probate Records, Will Book Vol. C (1790-1791) and Vol. D (1791-1796), with the
probate date recorded as June 1792. The application for this membership
cites Doc. 46, the inventory of William Saffer's estate dated 1792, and Docs.
47 and 48, respectively a decision and a pleading in the cases of *John Overall
v. Heirs of William Saffer* (1815) and *Isaac Overall v. Heirs of William
Saffer* (1823). That litigation persisted for more than three decades after
William's death, indicating that his estate included land of sufficient value to
generate sustained legal dispute, and that his heirs were known and present in
the county.

The surname Overall in both suits is not coincidental. William's land was
situated near Overall Run, and the Overalls were neighboring landowners. The
protracted litigation suggests a boundary or title dispute of the kind common to
land granted under the Fairfax system, where surveys were sometimes irregular
and adjacent claims overlapping.

### Daughter Hester Ann Saffer

The only child of William's identified in the available record is [Hester Ann
Saffer](https://www.wikitree.com/wiki/Saffron-5), born before 12 January 1792
in Shenandoah County, Virginia, who married Peter Lynch (1751-1823) in the
Shenandoah Valley in 1776. Through Hester Ann and Peter Lynch, the descent
passes to Tellitha Lynch, then to Andrew and Sophia Baumgardner, and onward
through the Bunnell and Williams lines to the present day.

The Daughters of the American Revolution have recognized William Saffer under
patriot number A211754, which is the basis for the WikiTree citation. This DAR
recognition is consistent with the land and census record evidence placing
William in the Valley throughout the Revolutionary period, though the precise
nature of his service or loyalty documentation in the DAR file has not been
independently reviewed.
